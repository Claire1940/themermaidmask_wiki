<Section icon={Target} title="Solving Mechanical and Logic Puzzles">

Puzzles in The Mermaid Mask sfb games are designed to be "in-world" mechanisms. This means they are physical locks or systems that characters have placed there for a reason, rather than abstract brain teasers. For example, the Serpent Cabinet in the Mess Hall is a mechanical lock that protects a bottle of Sun Tablets.

<Section icon={Swords} title="The Mind Game: Forming Logical Conclusions">

The "Mind Game" is the core narrative engine of The Mermaid Mask sfb games. It represents the detectives' thought processes as they reach a breakthrough. Instead of selecting a single answer from a list, you must construct a complete sentence using various subjects and connecting statements.

**How the Mind Game Works:**
- **Subject Selection**: Choose the person or object you are discussing (e.g., Magnus Mortuga).
- **Action/Connection**: Choose what they did or what happened to them (e.g., "unlocked the cauldron").
- **Result/Conclusion**: Choose the outcome or the implication (e.g., "and was killed by what was inside").

If your sentence is incorrect, Grimoire will read it aloud anyway, often resulting in humorous but logically flawed statements. The game provides feedback by telling you how many parts of your sentence are correct (e.g., "3 out of 4 pieces are right"). This encourages you to refine your logic rather than guess blindly.

| Mind Game Component | Purpose | Example |
| :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Subject** | The 'Who' or 'What' | Magnus Mortuga / The Wooden Boy |
| **Verb/Action** | The 'Did What' | Unlocked / Frame / Hidden |
| **Object/Location** | The 'Where' or 'To Whom' | The Cauldron / The Bulkhead Door |
| **Consequence** | The 'Result' | Was killed by what was inside |

<Callout type="info" title="Narrative Flow">
Mind Games often occur at the end of a chapter or after finding a critical piece of evidence. They are the only way to "lock in" your progress and move the story into the next phase.
</Callout>
